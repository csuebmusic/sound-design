phasor

A point rotating on a circle, tracing waves through its projections.

AAmplitude. The radius of the circle and the peak height of the wave. A larger A means the phasor arm is longer and the wave swings further from zero. In audio, amplitude corresponds to loudness.
2πOne full revolution in radians (= 360°). The factor 2π converts frequency in cycles per second into angular velocity in radians per second, so the phasor completes exactly f full turns every second.
fFrequency in Hz. Sets how fast the phasor spins. At f = 1 Hz the phasor completes one revolution per second, producing one wave cycle per second. Doubling f doubles the rotation speed and halves the wave period.
tTime in seconds. The product 2π·f·t is the accumulated angle — how far around the circle the phasor has traveled. As t grows, the wave unfolds to the right.
φPhase in degrees. Sets the starting angle at t = 0. At φ = 0° the tip starts at the rightmost point (sin = 0, cos = 1). At φ = 90° it starts at the top (sin = 1, cos = 0). Drag the phase slider and watch all three panels shift simultaneously — sin and cos always remain 90° apart.
jThe imaginary unit (√−1). In the complex exponential e^(jθ), the horizontal (real) axis carries the cosine and the vertical (imaginary) axis carries the sine. A rotating phasor encodes both projections simultaneously — this is why sine and cosine always travel in pairs throughout signal processing.

A·e^(jθ) = A·(cos(θ) + j·sin(θ)) — both projections on one circle. The real axis (horizontal) feeds the cosine track; the imaginary axis (vertical) feeds the sine track. In digital signal processing — z-transforms, filter poles and zeros, Fourier analysis — every calculation lives in this plane. Conjugate pole pairs exist because real-valued audio signals force the cosine and sine components to be symmetric: you cannot have one without the other.

sin(θ) + cos(θ) · the sum
A·sin(θ) + A·cos(θ) is itself a sinusoid at the same frequency, with amplitude A√2 and phase shifted by 45°. Adding two sinusoids of equal frequency always yields another sinusoid — the frequency never changes, only the amplitude and phase. This is the principle behind phasor addition and why the z-plane and Fourier analysis work: complex exponentials are closed under addition. Vary the phase slider and watch the sum (terra-cotta) shift and its peak amplitude remain A√2 regardless of phase.
