phase interference

Two sine waves, same frequency, independent amplitudes — drag the phase offset and watch constructive and destructive interference emerge.

real-world context

Two microphones on the same source at different distances. The farther mic receives the same sound slightly later — a time delay that translates directly into a phase offset at any given frequency.

When the two signals are mixed together, the phase offset causes comb filtering: some frequencies add constructively, others cancel destructively. The result is a hollow, colored sound with notches at regular frequency intervals.

The 3:1 rule is the standard fix: place the second mic at least three times further from the source than the first. This keeps the phase relationships benign across the audible spectrum.

The phase slider below is a stand-in for that path length difference. At 0° the mics are effectively at the same distance. At 180° they are half a wavelength apart — complete cancellation at that frequency.

phasor addition

Two phasor arrows tip-to-tail. The resultant (terra-cotta) is R. When they point the same direction (φ = 0°) R = A₁ + A₂. When they point opposite (φ = 180°) R = |A₁ − A₂|.
wave 1 · wave 2 · sum
wave 1 · A₁·sin(θ)
wave 2 · A₂·sin(θ+φ)
sum · R·sin(θ+ψ)
amplitude guide ±R
Drag the phase slider. At 0° the waves align and the sum reaches A₁ + A₂. At 180° they cancel to |A₁ − A₂|. With equal amplitudes, 180° produces complete silence. With unequal amplitudes, the quieter wave is subtracted from the louder — the difference survives. The resultant amplitude R = √(A₁² + A₂² + 2·A₁·A₂·cos(φ)).
