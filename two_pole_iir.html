two-pole IIR filter

A recursive filter with two feedback paths, a complex conjugate pole pair on the z-plane that creates resonance.

y[n] = g · x[n] + b₁ · y[n−1] − b₂ · y[n−2]
▷ multiplier (gain) · ⊕ summer · z⁻¹ one-sample delay · ● tap / junction

terminology

The two-pole filter inherits everything from the one-pole and adds one big new idea: complex conjugate pole pairs. This is what gives the filter its resonance — the ability to ring at a specific frequency.

y[n−1] and y[n−2] · two delays

The equation now references the previous TWO output samples, not just one. In the diagram you can see the delay chain: y[n] goes into the first z⁻¹ box and comes out as y[n−1]; that goes into the second z⁻¹ and comes out as y[n−2]. Two delays = two poles.

g, b₁, and b₂ are coefficients

Three numbers that define the filter. Same idea as the one-pole's g and r, but with one input gain and two feedback coefficients instead of one. Each plays a distinct role:

g input gain

Scales the incoming sample x[n] before it enters the summer. Same role as in the one-pole: it controls how loud the input arrives at the filter, but on its own doesn't shape the spectrum. Combined with b₁ and b₂ it determines the filter's overall output level.

b₁ first feedback coefficient

Scales the most recent previous output y[n−1]. It's the analog of r in the one-pole, except now there are two feedback paths working together. b₁ is computed from frequency and radius as b₁ = 2r·cos(ω₀).

b₂ second feedback coefficient

Scales the older previous output y[n−2]. Always positive: b₂ = r² (Cook Eq. 3.14). It is subtracted in the difference equation — that combination of adding b₁·y[n−1] and subtracting b₂·y[n−2] is what creates the oscillating, ringing behavior. The second feedback path is what allows complex poles to exist.

complex conjugate pole pair

In the one-pole filter the pole sat on the real axis (a number between −1 and +1). With two poles, they live as a mirror pair across the real axis (one above, one below) at the same distance from the origin. This pair is what creates resonance: the filter "rings" at a frequency determined by the angle of the poles. In musical practice you don't set b₁ and b₂ directly; instead you set the frequency and radius of the pole pair, and the filter computes the coefficients for you. These two parameters wear several hats:

freq resonant frequency = pole angle

The angle of the pole pair on the z-plane. Higher frequency rotates the poles further around the unit circle (away from the +1 DC point, toward the −1 Nyquist point). Musically, this is where the filter's peak sits: the frequency that gets emphasized.

radius pole distance from origin

How far the poles are from the center of the z-plane. Closer to the unit circle (radius near 1) = sharper resonance, longer ringing, more dramatic peak. Closer to the center (radius near 0) = broader, softer, almost no resonance. Stability requires radius < 1.

resonance · the filter's "voice"

A two-pole filter doesn't just shape spectrum; it oscillates. Hit it with a single impulse and the output rings like a struck bell, decaying exponentially. The ringing frequency is the resonant frequency; the decay rate depends on the radius. This is the foundation of formant filters, plucked-string models, and analog filter emulations.

group delay

What is group delay? The number of samples by which the filter shifts each frequency component in time. For a linear-phase filter (like the FIR moving average) every frequency is delayed by the same amount, and group delay is one number. For an IIR filter, group delay varies with frequency — the curve below is what that variation looks like.

For this two-pole filter: group delay peaks at the resonant frequency, where the filter rings. The peak grows steeply as radius approaches 1 — roughly as 1/(1−r), the same growth law that drives the magnitude peak. The curve is bell-shaped around the resonant frequency, narrower and taller as radius climbs. This is the perceptual side of resonance: a sharp resonator doesn't just emphasize a frequency band, it also delays that band relative to everything else. A short percussive input played through a sharp resonator sounds smeared because the resonant content arrives noticeably later than the wide-band attack — the filter stretches the struck moment out in time.

The negative regions (the curve dips below zero away from the resonant peak) are real, not a plotting error. They're roughly twice as deep as the one-pole's at the same radius — down to about −1 sample near instability. Depending on where the resonant frequency sits in the spectrum, negative regions can appear on one or both sides of the peak. Negative group delay is a property of how the filter reshapes wave envelopes, not a violation of cause and effect; it's part of why group delay needed its own name rather than being called "latency."

under the hood

z-plane · pole pair

What is the z-plane? A 2D map of complex numbers used to visualize what a filter does. The horizontal axis (Re) is the real part of z; the vertical axis (Im) is the imaginary part. The dashed circle is the unit circle, where |z| = 1, and it represents every frequency from DC (at z = +1) around through Nyquist (at z = −1) and back, with frequency tracked by angle. Filters are characterized by poles (where the transfer function blows up) and zeros (where it drops to zero); their positions on this plane encode the filter's entire behavior.

For the two-pole filter: the poles come as a complex conjugate pair, two pink X's mirroring each other across the horizontal axis. Their angle sets the resonant frequency; their distance from the center sets the resonance sharpness. Being off the real axis is exactly what gives the two-pole its ringing character, which the one-pole (stuck on the real line) cannot produce.

Re = real axis · Im = imaginary axis · dashed circle = unit circle (|z| = 1) · pink X = pole (two poles, mirrored across Re) · pole angle = frequency · distance from origin = radius · DC = 0 Hz · Nyq = Nyquist (SR/2)

impulse response · the filter rings

What is an impulse response? The output of the filter when the input is a single sample of value 1 (a click) followed by silence. Time runs left to right along the horizontal axis (n is the sample number); the vertical axis shows the output value at each sample. The impulse response is a complete fingerprint of the filter: knowing it tells you everything the filter can do to any other signal.

For the two-pole filter: the impulse response is an exponentially-decaying sinusoid. The filter rings at the resonant frequency while fading away. The amber envelope shows the decay rate (set by radius); the oscillation inside that envelope shows the ringing frequency. This is exactly how a struck object behaves, and it's the heart of physical-modeling synthesis.

n = sample number (discrete time axis) · teal stems = output samples · amber envelope = exponential decay (rate set by radius) · oscillation inside envelope = resonant frequency · window auto-scales with radius to show the full ring

in more depth

  • The transfer function in the z-domain is H(z) = g / (1 − b₁·z⁻¹ + b₂·z⁻²) (Cook Eq. 3.14). This is the recurrence y[n] = g·x[n] + b₁·y[n−1] − b₂·y[n−2] rewritten in the language of the z-transform. X(z) and Y(z) are the z-transforms of the input and output signals (a way of representing entire signals as a single function instead of one sample at a time); the feedback terms b₁·y[n−1] and b₂·y[n−2] become b₁·z⁻¹·Y(z) and b₂·z⁻²·Y(z) on the right side, then we solve for H(z) = Y(z)/X(z). The denominator factors as (1 − r·e^(jω₀)·z⁻¹)(1 − r·e^(−jω₀)·z⁻¹), making the two complex-conjugate poles explicit.
  • The coefficients are computed from radius r and resonant frequency f₀ as: b₁ = 2r·cos(ω₀) and b₂ = r² (Cook Eq. 3.14; b₂ is subtracted in the equation), where ω₀ = 2π·f₀ / SR. This places the two poles at z = r·e^(±jω₀) — same magnitude r, opposite angles ±ω₀. That symmetric placement is what guarantees the coefficients are real numbers (no imaginary part), so the filter can run on real-valued audio samples.
  • The impulse response is h[n] = (g/sin(ω₀)) · rⁿ · sin((n+1)·ω₀), an exponentially-decaying sinusoid. The rⁿ term is the envelope; the sin term is the ring. This is the same form as a struck bell, a plucked string in its decay phase, or any damped harmonic oscillator — a deep connection to physical systems.
  • Two ways to measure how long the filter rings: the time constant τ = −1 / (SR · ln(r)) seconds is when the envelope decays to 1/e (≈ 37%); the "impulse decay to 1%" metric in the status card above measures the same exponential decay further out, at 1% (≈ −40 dB). Same envelope, two thresholds. The 1% measure correlates well with how long the ring is audible to a listener.
  • Group delay for a two-pole is the sum of the two complex poles' contributions, since the denominator factors as (1 − r·e^(jω₀)·z⁻¹)(1 − r·e^(−jω₀)·z⁻¹) and the log of a product is a sum (so phase contributions add, and so do their derivatives). In the plotted frequency range [0, π] only the +ω₀ pole produces a visible spike — centered at the resonant frequency — while the −ω₀ pole contributes a shallow mostly-negative baseline. The observed peak height at ω = ω₀ grows roughly as 1/(1−r), comparable to how the magnitude peak grows. This frequency-dependent delay is the timing side of resonance: a short percussive input gets stretched in time at the resonant frequency, which is how a struck wineglass turns a quick tap into a long-ringing tone. FIR filters with symmetric coefficients sidestep this entirely (every frequency delayed by exactly (N−1)/2 samples), at the cost of needing many more taps.
  • Q (quality factor) measures how sharp the resonance is: Q ≈ ω₀ / (−2·ln r) when r is close to 1. Doubling Q halves the bandwidth and roughly doubles the ringing time. Q is the universal language of resonators — synth filters, pendulum clocks, RLC circuits, string instruments all use it.
  • Bandwidth (the width of the resonant peak at −3 dB) is approximately BW ≈ (1−r) · SR / π. As radius approaches 1, bandwidth approaches zero; the filter becomes a pure oscillator at f₀.
  • Peak gain at the resonant frequency is approximately g / ((1 − r) · |1 − r·e^(−j2ω₀)|). As r approaches 1, the first factor blows up — which is exactly the towering peak you see in the frequency response graph at high radius. In musical practice, audio filter designs often auto-normalize by scaling g so the peak stays at 0 dB regardless of radius.
  • Rolloff away from the resonant peak is −12 dB/octave on each side (twice as steep as a one-pole, because two poles instead of one). This is the foundation of steeper filter design: cascade more poles for sharper rolloffs (4-pole = −24 dB/oct = the classic Moog ladder).
  • This is the building block for resonant filter banks (formant synthesis, Karplus-Strong plucked-string models, modal synthesis of bells and bars). One two-pole gives you a single resonance; sum a handful of them at different frequencies and you get a rich, multi-modal sound.

try this

  1. Set radius = 0.99 and start playback. The filter is so narrow that it picks out a tiny slice of the noise spectrum at the resonant frequency, transforming the hiss into a pitched tone. Now slowly sweep the frequency slider; you're hearing the resonant pitch glide. The narrower the resonance, the more like a sine wave it sounds.
  2. With frequency set to the middle of the audible band (around 1 kHz), compare radius = 0.5, 0.9, 0.99, 0.999. Very different character: gentle spectral tilt → emerging bump → clearly pitched resonance → near-pure oscillator. At low radius the poles' effect smears across a broad region; at high radius it concentrates into a sharp peak. This is the Q sweep — the same control sweep that defines the analog synth filter sound.
  3. Set frequency = 850 Hz, radius = 0.97 (a crude vowel "ah" formant). Then jump to frequency = 2700 Hz, radius = 0.97 (closer to "ee"). You're hearing single-formant approximations of how the human vocal tract shapes sound — real vowels need 3+ formants summed together.
  4. Watch the z-plane while you drag the frequency slider: the two poles rotate around the origin together, always mirroring across the real axis. Now drag radius: they slide toward or away from the unit circle while staying at the same angle.
  5. Drag the radius slider above 1 (into the unstable region). The poles cross outside the unit circle and the filter explodes — audio mutes, impulse response grows unbounded. Same lesson as one-pole, just with a pair of poles instead of one.
  6. Set radius = 0.9, then sweep frequency across the full slider range. Watch the peak height in the frequency response graph as you sweep. It changes! The peak is highest near the middle of the audible band and grows dramatically at the extremes (near DC or Nyquist). This is the second factor in the peak-gain formula — the |1 − r·e^(−j2ω₀)| term — which depends on frequency. Two-pole peak gain isn't constant the way one-pole is.
