one-pole IIR filter

The simplest recursive digital filter, a single feedback path with one pole on the real axis of the z-plane.

y[n] = g · x[n] + r · y[n−1]
▷ multiplier (gain) · ⊕ summer · z⁻¹ one-sample delay · ● tap / junction

terminology

Before touching the sliders, here's what the symbols in the equation actually mean. The whole filter behavior comes from these few ideas.

x[n] input · y[n] output

The notation [n] means "the n-th sample." So x[n] is the current input sample, and y[n−1] is the previous output sample. The filter computes one output sample at a time, from left to right.

z⁻¹ one-sample delay

In the block diagram you'll see a box labeled z⁻¹. It's the unit delay: feed it y[n] and what comes out is y[n−1], the previous sample, held for one tick. The z notation comes from the z-transform (a math tool for analyzing digital filters); the −1 exponent means "delay by one sample." So y[n−1] in the equation and the z⁻¹ box in the diagram are the same idea in two notations.

g and r are coefficients

Coefficients are just numbers, the recipe that defines the filter. Change these two numbers and you change everything the filter does. Each one plays a different role:

g input gain

Scales the incoming sample x[n] before it enters the summer. With g = 1, the input passes through full-strength; with g = 0.2, the input is attenuated to 20% of its level. g is what controls how loud the input arrives at the filter; on its own it doesn't shape the spectrum, but combined with feedback (r) it determines the filter's overall output level.

r feedback amount

Scales how much of the previous output sample y[n−1] is fed back and added to the current input. r is the heart of the filter: it sets the cutoff frequency, controls the decay time, and determines stability. r wears three hats — it's worth seeing all of them:

r is feedback. Because the equation uses the previous output (y[n−1]), the filter loops back on itself. This is what makes it recursive, and what puts the "infinite" in IIR (Infinite Impulse Response). Without feedback the filter would forget; with it, the filter remembers.

r is also a pole. A pole is a location on the z-plane where the filter "stores energy", a point the filter wants to dwell on. Musically: poles near the edge of the unit circle make the filter ring and shape sound strongly; poles near zero make the filter pass sound through almost untouched. In the one-pole filter, the value of r is the pole's location. Same number, two views: a feedback amount in the equation, and a point on a 2D map.

r determines stability · |r| < 1. If the size of r stays under 1, the feedback loop eventually decays to silence after each input; the filter is stable. If |r| hits 1 or higher, the loop amplifies itself forever and the output grows without bound. The filter explodes.

group delay

What is group delay? The number of samples by which the filter shifts each frequency component in time. For a linear-phase filter (like the FIR moving average) every frequency is delayed by the same amount, and group delay is one number. For an IIR filter, group delay varies with frequency — the curve below is what that variation looks like.

For this one-pole filter: group delay is largest where the filter's magnitude peaks — at DC for positive r (lowpass), at Nyquist for negative r (highpass) — and decreases smoothly as you move away from that peak. The peak height depends on how close the pole sits to the unit circle: at r = 0.9 the peak is about 9 samples; at r = 0.99 it reaches 99 samples. This is exactly the transient smearing that distinguishes IIR from FIR: the bass and treble of a drum hit are delayed by different amounts, so they no longer arrive at the output at the same moment.

The negative region (the curve dips below zero past the cutoff) is real, not a plotting error. For a lowpass setting it appears roughly past f = SR·acos(r)/(2π), and as r approaches 1 it grows to cover most of the spectrum above the peak. The dip is shallow — at most around half a sample — but it's a genuine property of how the filter reshapes wave envelopes, not a violation of cause and effect. This is part of why group delay needed its own name rather than being called "latency."

under the hood

z-plane · pole position

What is the z-plane? A 2D map of complex numbers used to visualize what a filter does. The horizontal axis (Re) is the real part of z; the vertical axis (Im) is the imaginary part. The dashed circle is the unit circle, where |z| = 1, and it represents every frequency from DC (at z = +1) around through Nyquist (at z = −1) and back, with frequency tracked by angle. Filters are characterized by poles (where the transfer function blows up) and zeros (where it drops to zero); their positions on this plane encode the filter's entire behavior.

For the one-pole filter: there is exactly one pole, and it's locked to the horizontal axis between −1 (Nyquist) and +1 (DC). When you move r, you're sliding that pole along this line. Positive r places the pole near +1 and emphasizes DC (lowpass); negative r places it near −1 and emphasizes Nyquist (highpass). How close the pole sits to the unit circle determines how sharp the filter is.

Re = real axis · Im = imaginary axis · dashed circle = unit circle (|z| = 1) · pink X = pole · amber line = real-axis slice where one-pole poles live · DC = 0 Hz · Nyq = Nyquist (SR/2)

impulse response · the filter's fingerprint

What is an impulse response? The output of the filter when the input is a single sample of value 1 (a click) followed by silence. Time runs left to right along the horizontal axis (n is the sample number); the vertical axis shows the output value at each sample. The impulse response is a complete fingerprint of the filter: knowing it tells you everything the filter can do to any other signal.

For the one-pole filter: the feedback loop multiplies the initial impulse by r, then by r again, then again, sample after sample. Each stem is g · rⁿ, a pure exponential decay. Positive r gives a monotonic decay; negative r gives a sign-alternating decay at the Nyquist rate.

in more depth

  • The transfer function in the z-domain is H(z) = g / (1 − r·z⁻¹). This is the recurrence y[n] = g·x[n] + r·y[n−1] rewritten in the language of the z-transform. X(z) and Y(z) are the z-transforms of the input and output signals (a way of representing entire signals as a single function instead of one sample at a time); the sample-domain feedback term r·y[n−1] becomes r·z⁻¹·Y(z) on the right side, then we solve for H(z) = Y(z)/X(z). Same filter, two views — one tells you how to compute samples one at a time, the other tells you how the filter behaves across all frequencies at once. The denominator equals zero when z = r; that's why r is called the pole.
  • The frequency response formula is |H(e^jω)| = g / √(1 − 2r·cos(ω) + r²). The notation e^jω means "evaluate the transfer function on the unit circle of the z-plane" — that's how you get from poles in the abstract to the curve you see on screen. The pole sits at angle 0 (the +1 side of the unit circle) for positive r, so DC gets the maximum boost; for negative r the pole sits at angle π (the −1 side, Nyquist), so highs get boosted instead.
  • The impulse response is exactly h[n] = g · rⁿ for n ≥ 0. This is why IIR filters have "infinite" impulse responses: rn only reaches zero in the limit, never exactly.
  • Two ways to measure the filter's memory: the time constant τ = −1 / (SR · ln|r|) seconds is when the impulse decays to 1/e (≈ 37%); the "impulse decay to 1%" metric in the status card above measures the same thing further out, at 1% (≈ −40 dB). Same exponential decay, different cutoff thresholds — pick whichever is more useful for the situation.
  • Rolloff: exactly −6 dB/octave beyond the cutoff (equivalently, −20 dB/decade). This is the defining slope of any first-order filter. Cascading two one-poles doubles the rolloff to −12 dB/oct (second-order); cascading four gives −24 dB/oct, the classic Moog ladder slope.
  • Group delay for a one-pole has the closed form τ(ω) = (r·cos(ω) − r²) / (1 − 2r·cos(ω) + r²), derived by differentiating the phase response φ(ω) = −arctan(r·sin(ω) / (1 − r·cos(ω))). Unlike a linear-phase FIR (the moving average's group delay is one number for all frequencies), the IIR's delay varies with frequency: tall near the magnitude peak, near-zero everywhere else, and slightly negative in narrow bands. That frequency-dependent delay is what smears transients — the bass and treble of a percussive sound no longer arrive simultaneously at the output. FIR filters with symmetric coefficients sidestep this entirely (every frequency delayed by exactly (N−1)/2 samples), at the cost of needing many more taps for the same sharpness. This trade is why mastering EQs offer "linear phase" modes — they swap the IIR's frequency-dependent smearing for the FIR's bigger latency and pre-ringing.
  • The peak gain is g / (1 − |r|). For positive r this peak sits at DC; for negative r it sits at Nyquist. As |r| approaches 1, the denominator approaches 0 and the peak shoots up — which is exactly the towering peak you see in the frequency response graph at extreme settings (easily +40 dB at |r| ≈ 0.998 with g = 0.2, and higher still as |r| gets closer to 1). Audio filter designs often auto-normalize by setting g = 1 − |r| so the peak stays at 1 (0 dB) regardless of r.

try this

  1. Set r = 0.99 and start playback. The filter is so narrow that it picks out a tiny slice of the noise spectrum near DC, transforming the hiss into a low rumble. Now lower r toward 0 — hear the full broadband noise return as the filter widens. The narrower the filter, the more pitched and tonal the noise sounds.
  2. Compare r = 0.9 vs r = −0.9. Same filter strength, opposite spectral character: bass-heavy vs. treble-heavy. Note how the response curve mirrors itself across the spectrum.
  3. Drag the r slider past ±1 in either direction (into the unstable regions at the far ends). Watch the impulse response (it grows without bound) and the metrics (UNSTABLE). Audio is muted automatically; this is what "outside the unit circle" sounds like in theory.
  4. Set r = 0.95 and read the cutoff in the metrics. Now try r = 0.5. Same filter topology, vastly different cutoff, controlled by a single number.
  5. Set g = 0.2 and slowly push r from 0.9 → 0.99 → 0.998. Watch the peak of the curve climb above 0 dB toward +40 dB. Push further to 0.9998 and the peak climbs another 20 dB toward +60 — the chart's +80 dB ceiling lets you see the peak explode smoothly as r approaches 1. Now manually set g = 1 − |r| at each step (≈ 0.1, 0.01, 0.002, 0.0002) and watch the peak snap back to 0 dB. This is auto-normalization, by hand.
