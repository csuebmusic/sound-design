2-zero FIR filter

A non-recursive filter with three feedforward taps, producing a complex conjugate zero pair on the z-plane. At r = 1 the zeros sit on the unit circle and the filter becomes a pure notch; at r < 1 the zeros sit inside and the filter combines a partial dip at f_zero with a broader spectral tilt.

y[n] = g · x[n] + a₁ · x[n−1] + a₂ · x[n−2]
▷ multiplier (gain) · ⊕ summer · z⁻¹ one-sample delay · ● tap / junction

terminology

The 2-zero FIR is the feedforward mirror of the two-pole IIR. Where the IIR placed a conjugate pole pair to create resonance, this filter places a conjugate zero pair. Same geometric vocabulary (angle, radius, unit circle), always stable. This filter is a pure notch only at r = 1. At r < 1 the zeros sit inside the unit circle and the filter combines a partial dip at f_zero with a broader spectral tilt across the full band.

x[n−1] and x[n−2] · two input delays

The delay chain runs through x: x[n] into the first z⁻¹ gives x[n−1]; a second z⁻¹ gives x[n−2]. Each delayed copy is scaled by its own coefficient and summed. The output is never fed back.

g, a₁, and a₂ are coefficients

Three numbers that define the filter completely. All feedforward: each multiplies a delayed copy of the input. Each plays a distinct role:

g current-sample coefficient

Scales the most recent input x[n]. In the IIR tools g sat in front of the whole recurrence as an overall gain; here it's also the first tap of the FIR, so it's part of what shapes the filter, not just a level control. Sliding g affects the DC gain, the Nyquist gain, and (mildly) the zero locations.

a₁ first delay coefficient

Scales the one-sample-delayed input x[n−1]. Computed from the zero frequency and radius as a₁ = −2r·g·cos(ω₀). The negative sign comes from the polynomial-root identity: to place zeros at z = r·e^(±jω₀) the coefficient of z⁻¹ has to be −(sum of the zeros). Students often see a₁ as negative for low zero frequencies and positive for high ones (past SR/4).

a₂ second delay coefficient

Scales the two-sample-delayed input x[n−2]. Computed as a₂ = g·r²; always the same sign as g, always smaller in magnitude for r < 1. At r = 1 we get a₂ = g exactly, making the coefficient sequence symmetric (g, a₁, g), which turns the filter into a pure-notch linear-phase filter.

complex conjugate zero pair

The two values of z where the filter's output drops to a minimum (or to exactly zero if the zeros sit on the unit circle). For real coefficients, zeros must be real or come in mirror pairs across the real axis (one above, one below, same distance from origin). This mirror pair is the headline object of this tool. You don't set a₁ and a₂ directly; you set the frequency and radius of the zero pair, and the filter derives the coefficients:

freq zero frequency = zero angle

The angle of the zero pair on the z-plane, expressed in Hz for easier intuition. Musically, this is the frequency that gets attenuated: where the notch or dip sits. Sliding it rotates the zeros around the unit circle (if r = 1) or around a smaller circle (if r < 1), staying as a mirror pair across the real axis.

radius zero distance from origin

How far the zeros are from the center of the z-plane. Only at r = 1 does the filter become a pure notch: the zeros sit exactly on the unit circle and the magnitude response touches −∞ dB at the zero frequency, while staying flat on both sides. At r < 1 the zeros pull inside, and two things happen at once: the dip at f_zero becomes shallower (about −19 dB at r = 0.9, −38 dB at r = 0.99), and the whole spectrum gains a tilt because the coefficient sequence (g, a₁, g·r²) is asymmetric. The tilt is mild when f_zero sits in the middle of the audible band and dramatic when f_zero sits near DC or Nyquist. So the slider controls not only notch depth but how close the filter gets to being a true notch at all.

at r = 1 · the pure notch case

When zeros sit exactly on the unit circle, the coefficient sequence becomes (g, −2g·cos(ω₀), g), which is symmetric. Three consequences: (1) the filter has linear phase, with group delay exactly 1 sample at every frequency except at f_zero itself; (2) the magnitude response is flat at g everywhere except the single-sample-wide notch at f_zero; (3) the filter has the cleanest possible "kill one frequency, pass everything else" character, which is why this is the textbook starting point for notch filters.

the filter's "voice"

A 2-zero FIR doesn't ring; it silences selectively. Hit it with an impulse and the output is three samples long, then dead. In the r = 1 limit this is the foundation of textbook notch filters (killing 50/60 Hz hum, removing a specific tone). At r < 1 the filter produces a broader range of behaviors: shallow dips combined with spectral tilts. Three multiplies per sample, unconditionally stable.

group delay

What is group delay? The number of samples by which the filter shifts each frequency component in time. For a linear-phase filter (like the moving-average FIR from the sibling tool) every frequency is delayed by the same amount, and group delay is one number. But not every FIR is linear-phase, and this one isn't, except in a very specific limit.

For this FIR: group delay has a dramatic negative spike at the zero frequency. It's the mirror image of the two-pole IIR's positive peak at its resonant frequency: same narrow band of large excursion, same 1/(1−r) growth law, same root cause (|H(ω)| getting small in a narrow region), with the sign flipped. Zeros subtract phase near f_zero just like poles add phase near f_res. At r = 0.9 the spike reaches about −9 samples; at r = 0.99 it reaches about −99; at r = 1 it is undefined (the group-delay formula has a 0/0 at the notch, which the chart handles by returning 0 at that point).

Away from the zero frequency, group delay sits near +1 sample across the rest of the spectrum (especially at high r). That's exactly the mean tap position (N−1)/2 = 1 for a 3-tap filter (the same linear-phase formula you saw in the moving-average FIR), because as r → 1, the coefficients approach the symmetric form (g, a₁, g). So this filter is approximately linear-phase everywhere except in a narrow band around the notch, where the geometry of the zero on the unit circle breaks symmetry and produces the spike. Negative group delay here, like in the IIR, doesn't violate causality; it's a property of how the filter reshapes wave envelopes.

under the hood

z-plane · zero pair

What is the z-plane? A 2D map of complex numbers used to visualize what a filter does. The horizontal axis (Re) is the real part of z; the vertical axis (Im) is the imaginary part. The dashed circle is the unit circle, where |z| = 1, and it represents every frequency from DC (at z = +1) around through Nyquist (at z = −1) and back, with frequency tracked by angle. Filters are characterized by poles (where the transfer function goes to infinity) and zeros (where it drops to zero); their positions on this plane encode the filter's entire behavior.

For this FIR: there are no interesting poles (just a trivial double pole at z = 0 that we don't draw), only zeros. The two zeros come as a complex conjugate pair, drawn as pink rings, not the X-marks used for poles in the IIR tools. Their angle sets the zero frequency; their distance from the center sets how deep the dip is at that frequency. At radius = 1 the zeros sit exactly on the unit circle (which is the set of points the frequency response actually traces), so the filter's output drops to exactly zero at that frequency: a true notch with flat response on either side. At radius < 1 the zeros sit inside the circle, producing a shallower dip and also tilting the overall spectrum.

Re = real axis · Im = imaginary axis · dashed circle = unit circle (|z| = 1) · pink ring = zero (two zeros, mirrored across Re) · zero angle = frequency · distance from origin = radius · DC = 0 Hz · Nyq = Nyquist (SR/2)

impulse response · three samples, then silence

What is an impulse response? The output of the filter when the input is a single sample of value 1 (a click) followed by silence. Time runs left to right along the horizontal axis (n is the sample number); the vertical axis shows the output value at each sample. The impulse response is a complete fingerprint of the filter: knowing it tells you everything the filter can do to any other signal.

For this FIR: the impulse response IS the coefficient list. Three samples (h[0] = g, h[1] = a₁, h[2] = a₂), then silence. That's the defining "finite" property of finite impulse response: feed the filter a click, count to three, and the output is dead. Compare to the two-pole IIR, whose impulse response is an exponentially-decaying sinusoid that only approaches silence over many samples.

in more depth

  • The transfer function in the z-domain is H(z) = g + a₁·z⁻¹ + a₂·z⁻², a polynomial in z⁻¹. This is the recurrence y[n] = g·x[n] + a₁·x[n−1] + a₂·x[n−2] rewritten in the language of the z-transform. Since there's no feedback, there's no denominator: all three coefficients appear directly in the numerator. The zeros of the filter are the roots of that polynomial: the two values of z where H(z) = 0; those roots encode the filter's entire spectral behavior.
  • The coefficients are computed from radius r, zero frequency f₀, and gain g as: a₁ = −2g·r·cos(ω₀) and a₂ = g·r², where ω₀ = 2π·f₀ / SR. This comes from factoring H(z) as g · (1 − r·e^(jω₀)·z⁻¹)(1 − r·e^(−jω₀)·z⁻¹), which places the two zeros at z = r·e^(±jω₀). Expanding and collecting: the z⁰ term is g, the z⁻¹ term is −g(r·e^(jω₀) + r·e^(−jω₀)) = −2g·r·cos(ω₀), and the z⁻² term is g·r·e^(jω₀)·r·e^(−jω₀) = g·r². The cancellation of imaginary parts is exactly what the conjugate-pair structure guarantees, which is why we use a pair of zeros (not a single complex zero) to keep the coefficients real.
  • The impulse response IS the coefficient list. Hit this filter with a single x[0] = 1, and the outputs are y[0] = g, y[1] = a₁, y[2] = a₂, then zero. The filter retains nothing past N−1 = 2 samples. Compare with the two-pole IIR, whose impulse response is an exponentially-decaying sinusoid; feedback keeps the output alive for hundreds or thousands of samples.
  • DC gain and Nyquist gain have simple closed forms. At DC (ω = 0), e⁻ʲʷ = 1, so H(1) = g + a₁ + a₂. At Nyquist (ω = π), e⁻ʲπ = −1 so e⁻ʲ²π = 1, giving H(−1) = g − a₁ + a₂. Since all coefficients are real, both gains are real numbers (not just magnitudes) and can even be negative, which corresponds to phase inversion at that frequency. At f_zero = 12 kHz (= SR/4) DC and Nyquist gain are equal; move the zero away from there and they diverge.
  • Notch depth at the zero frequency has the closed form |H(ω₀)| = g · (1 − r) · |1 − r·e^(−j2ω₀)|. The (1 − r) factor is the dominant one: it drives the dip depth toward zero as r → 1, which is why r controls the slider's "how deep" behavior so cleanly. The second factor depends on where f_zero sits in the spectrum. At ω₀ = π/2 (f_zero = SR/4 = 12 kHz) the second factor equals 1 + r, giving notch depth g · (1 − r²); at extreme frequencies (near DC or Nyquist) the second factor approaches (1 − r), giving depth g · (1 − r)², deeper by a factor of (1 + r)/(1 − r). This is why the notch-depth metric varies strongly with f_zero at fixed r.
  • This FIR is unconditionally stable. With no feedback, the output at every sample is bounded by three multiplies of the current input — zeros can sit anywhere in the z-plane without risk. The trade-off is sharpness: a BiQuad (poles + zeros together) can achieve a much narrower notch for the same coefficient count, because its poles amplify the effect of its zeros.
  • Linear phase happens only at r = 1. For a symmetric coefficient sequence (h[0], h[1], h[0]) the filter has exactly linear phase, with group delay (N−1)/2 = 1 sample at every frequency. Our coefficients are (g, a₁, g·r²), symmetric only when g = g·r², which forces r = 1. At r < 1 the sequence is asymmetric and the filter is non-linear-phase in general. Interestingly, this deviation is concentrated in a narrow band around the notch: everywhere else on the spectrum group delay sits near τ = 1. So the filter is "almost" linear phase except in the neighborhood of the feature it's actually creating.
  • Group delay has the closed form τ(ω) = Re[N(ω)/D(ω)] where N(ω) = a₁·e⁻ʲʷ + 2·a₂·e⁻ʲ²ʷ and D(ω) = g + a₁·e⁻ʲʷ + a₂·e⁻ʲ²ʷ = H(e^jω). The negative spike at ω = ω₀ is driven by D(ω) getting small in a narrow band, the same narrow band where the magnitude dip lives. This is the timing mirror of the two-pole IIR's positive spike at its resonance: both arise from |H| being small in a narrow region, but with opposite signs because zeros subtract phase where poles add it.
  • Practical use: notch filters. Setting r close to 1 and f_zero = 60 Hz removes power-line hum from a recording. Setting f_zero = 50 Hz does the same in Europe. More generally, any time you need to kill a single narrow-band interfering frequency without disturbing much else in the spectrum, a 2-zero FIR with r ≈ 0.97–0.99 is a first-choice building block.
  • Building toward the BiQuad. This filter is the numerator of the general second-order IIR: H(z) = (g + a₁·z⁻¹ + a₂·z⁻²) / (1 − b₁·z⁻¹ − b₂·z⁻²). Combining our 2-zero FIR feedforward with the two-pole IIR's feedback gives the BiQuad, the most important single second-order filter in audio DSP, because with free choice of zeros and poles it can realize any lowpass, highpass, bandpass, notch, peaking, or shelving response. The BiQuad is the natural next step after these three tools.

try this

  1. Set f_zero = 1 kHz, g = 1, start playback. Slowly sweep radius from 0 to 1 and watch the frequency response panel. Two things happen at once. The dip at 1 kHz deepens (that's what's easy to see). The whole curve changes shape too: at r = 0.5 it's a rising slope with a small dip, at r = 0.9 the slope is less, and at r = 1 the rest of the spectrum is flat and the dip reaches the chart floor. The slider controls not just depth but how close the filter gets to being a true notch.
  2. Set f_zero = 60 Hz, r = 0.98, g = 1: a classic power-line hum rejection setting (50 Hz in Europe instead). At r close to 1 the filter is essentially a notch: the dip at 60 Hz reaches about −30 dB while the rest of the spectrum is close to flat. This is what a DAW's "notch" EQ band does in the low-frequency range. Now try r = 0.5 at the same f_zero: the dip shrinks and a rising slope appears because the filter is no longer a pure notch.
  3. Compare (f_zero = 5 kHz, r = 0.5) and (f_zero = 5 kHz, r = 0.99). At r = 0.5 the filter has a broad tilt from roughly −7 dB at DC rising through +11 dB at Nyquist, with a small dip at 5 kHz; at r = 0.99 the whole spectrum is flat near 0 dB except for a deep dip at 5 kHz. Same f_zero, same pair of zeros on the z-plane, just at different distances from the unit circle, and the behavior is completely different.
  4. At r = 0.9, g = 1, compare f_zero = 3 kHz (cos ω₀ > 0 → a₁ negative) with f_zero = 20 kHz (cos ω₀ < 0 → a₁ positive). Watch the impulse response panel: same magnitudes for all three coefficients, but the middle tap flips sign. This is the FIR version of the sign-flip duality you saw in the moving-average / differentiator tool; the sign pattern determines whether the dip sits below or above SR/4.
  5. Watch the z-plane while sweeping radius: the two zeros slide along a radial line toward the unit circle, staying at the same angle. At r = 1 the zeros sit exactly on the unit circle and the response touches −∞ dB at that frequency. Now sweep frequency while holding r = 1: the zero pair rotates around the circle as a mirrored pair.
  6. Set f_zero = 1 kHz, r = 0.5 first, look at the group-delay chart: a shallow dip near 1 kHz on an otherwise near-flat curve. Now bump r to 0.95, then to 0.99. The negative spike deepens enormously: from about −2 samples at r = 0.5, to −20 at r = 0.95, to −99 at r = 0.99. The group-delay spike grows as 1/(1−r), the same law as the IIR's resonant peak but with the sign flipped.
  7. With r = 1, g = 1, slowly sweep f_zero from 100 Hz to 20 kHz and listen. You're dragging a narrow silence through the noise, the perceptual opposite of the two-pole IIR's resonance sweep (which drags a narrow tone through the noise). A bank of these, tuned to specific frequencies, is the basis of inverse formant processing and spectral-hole effects.
