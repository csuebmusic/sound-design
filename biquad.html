BiQuad filter

Two poles and two zeros: the second-order pole/zero filter (Cook Eq. 3.11/3.14). The two-pole IIR and the 2-zero FIR combined into a single structure, and the most common building block in audio DSP.

y(n) = g·(x(n) + a₁·x(n−1) + a₂·x(n−2)) + b₁·y(n−1) − b₂·y(n−2)
■ feedback: b₁, b₂ → ⊕₁  ·  ■ feedforward: a₁, a₂ → ⊕₂  ·  ▷ multiplier · ⊕ summer · z⁻¹ one-sample delay

terminology

The BiQuad combines the 2-zero FIR feedforward section (teal) with the two-pole IIR feedback section (crimson) into a single structure. The key insight from LTI theory: any LTI system can be built from these components in any combination. The BiQuad is the most useful second-order combination: two poles for resonance, two zeros for anti-resonance, independently tunable.

the two sections
feedforward · zeros
freq_z zero frequency

The angle of the zero pair on the z-plane, the frequency the filter attenuates. Derived as ω_z = 2π·freq_z / SR.

rz zero radius

How far the zeros sit from the origin. At rz = 1 the zeros land on the unit circle: a true notch (−∞ dB) at freq_z. At rz < 1 a shallower dip. Coefficients: a₁ = −2rz·cos(ω_z), a₂ = rz².

feedback · poles
freq_p pole frequency

The angle of the pole pair on the z-plane, the frequency the filter amplifies. Derived as ω_p = 2π·freq_p / SR.

rp pole radius

How far the poles sit from the origin. Must stay strictly less than 1 for stability. As rp → 1 the resonance peak sharpens toward infinity. Coefficients: b₁ = 2rp·cos(ω_p), b₂ = rp².

g overall gain

Scales the entire feedforward sum before it enters the summer (Cook Eq. 3.11: g sits before the tap, factored over all three feedforward terms). It does not affect zero or pole locations, only the overall output level.

transfer function

In the z-domain: H(z) = g·(1 + a₁·z⁻¹ + a₂·z⁻²) / (1 − b₁·z⁻¹ + b₂·z⁻²). The numerator is the 2-zero FIR; the denominator is the two-pole IIR. Zeros are roots of the numerator, poles are roots of the denominator. Their interaction shapes the full frequency response: the zero can cancel or sculpt the pole's resonance, and vice versa.

when freq_z = freq_p

Setting both frequencies to the same value is instructive. If rz < rp the pole dominates: a resonance peak. If rz > rp the zero dominates: a notch. If rz = rp they cancel exactly: flat response. This is the core of parametric EQ, a peak and a notch at the same frequency, controlled by the radius ratio.

group delay

Group delay for the BiQuad combines the negative spike from the zeros (as seen in the 2-zero FIR tool) with the positive peak from the poles (as seen in the two-pole IIR tool). Near the pole frequency, group delay rises sharply; near the zero frequency, it dips sharply. When the two frequencies overlap, the effects interact: canceling, reinforcing, or producing complex shapes depending on the radius settings.

The formula is the same Re[N/D] form as the other tools, but now D = H(e^jω) for the full BiQuad transfer function, and N includes contributions from both feedforward and feedback coefficients.

under the hood

z-plane · poles and zeros

For the BiQuad: both poles (crimson ×) and zeros (teal ○) appear simultaneously. Zeros sit at radius rz, angle ±ω_z; poles at radius rp, angle ±ω_p. The frequency response traces the unit circle; at each angle the response magnitude is the product of distances to the zeros divided by the product of distances to the poles. Move a zero onto the unit circle and the response touches zero there. Move a pole close to the circle and the response peaks sharply.

○ ring = zero pair (teal) · × cross = pole pair (crimson) · dashed circle = unit circle

impulse response

For the BiQuad: the impulse response is an exponentially decaying sinusoid shaped by both the poles and zeros. The poles govern the decay rate and ringing frequency; the zeros sculpt the amplitude envelope, canceling specific frequency components or adding a brief initial transient. Unlike the pure two-pole IIR (which rings smoothly) or the pure 2-zero FIR (which terminates in 3 samples), the BiQuad rings and terminates at a rate controlled by rp.

in more depth

  • The transfer function is H(z) = g·(1 + a₁·z⁻¹ + a₂·z⁻²) / (1 − b₁·z⁻¹ + b₂·z⁻²) (Cook Eq. 3.12). The numerator is the 2-zero FIR; the denominator is the two-pole IIR. Together they let zeros and poles be placed anywhere in the z-plane (subject to conjugate-pair constraints for real coefficients). The BiQuad is the most compact second-order filter that can realize both resonance and anti-resonance simultaneously.
  • The frequency response magnitude is |H(e^jω)| = g · |numerator(ω)| / |denominator(ω)|. Geometrically, this is the product of distances from e^jω to each zero, divided by the product of distances to each pole, scaled by g. This geometric picture (poles push the response up, zeros pull it down) is the foundation for understanding any LTI filter.
  • When freq_z = freq_p: the zero and pole are at the same angle. If rz = rp, the numerator and denominator are identical and H(z) = g, perfectly flat response. If rz > rp, the zero wins (notch at that frequency). If rz < rp, the pole wins (peak). This is parametric EQ in its mathematical essence: a bump or dip at a chosen frequency, controlled by the radius ratio.
  • Setting rz = 0 and rp = 0 collapses the BiQuad to a simple gain stage (a₁ = a₂ = b₁ = b₂ = 0, output = g·x(n)). Setting rz = 0 gives a pure two-pole IIR. Setting rp = 0 gives a pure 2-zero FIR. The BiQuad is the most general second-order case that contains all of these as special cases.
  • Stability: only rp governs stability: the poles must stay inside the unit circle (rp < 1). Zeros can sit anywhere, including on or outside the unit circle, without affecting stability. This is why the zero radius slider goes to 1.0 while the pole radius caps at 0.999.
  • Group delay is the sum of the group delay contributions from the numerator and denominator. Near freq_p it peaks positively (same spike as the two-pole IIR); near freq_z it dips negatively (same spike as the 2-zero FIR). When the two frequencies are the same, the spikes partially cancel, which is one reason notch EQ using poles + zeros (BiQuad) has better phase behavior than pure FIR notches.
  • Building toward larger systems: any higher-order filter can be implemented as a cascade of BiQuad sections (Cook §3.11). The four-pole filter of Figure 3.15 is just two BiQuads in series. This cascade factorization is why the BiQuad is so central: it is the universal second-order building block from which arbitrary LTI systems are assembled.

try this

  1. Start with rz = 1, rp = 0.9, and set freq_z = freq_p to the same frequency. Watch the frequency response: the pole creates a peak, the zero creates a notch at the exact same spot. Slowly pull freq_z away from freq_p; the notch separates from the peak and the combined shape becomes a shelving or bandpass curve.
  2. Set rz = 0 (zeros collapse to origin): the tool becomes a pure two-pole IIR. Set rp = 0 (poles collapse): it becomes a pure 2-zero FIR. Compare to the sibling tools; the frequency response, z-plane, and impulse response should all match exactly.
  3. Set freq_p = 1 kHz, rp = 0.99 (sharp resonance). Then sweep rz from 0 to 1 at freq_z = 1 kHz. Watch the peak shrink as the zero radius grows; at rz = rp = 0.99 the notch exactly cancels the peak. Past rz = 0.99 the notch wins. This is the zero-pole cancellation principle.
  4. Try freq_z = 60 Hz, rz = 1.0, freq_p = 200 Hz, rp = 0.8. This approximates a notch (hum removal at 60 Hz) combined with a low-frequency boost, a common broadcast EQ curve. Sweep freq_p and listen to how the low-end character changes while the 60 Hz null stays fixed.
  5. Watch the z-plane while sweeping freq_z and freq_p independently. The zero pair (teal rings) rotates around the circle at its own radius; the pole pair (crimson ×) rotates at its own. When they cross each other's angles, the frequency response changes abruptly, the geometry of cancelation and reinforcement made visible.
  6. Set rp = 0.999 (near-infinite Q resonance). Set g very low (0.01) to keep the output from clipping. Sweep freq_p slowly from 100 Hz to 5 kHz with audio playing; you're sweeping a nearly-sinusoidal resonator through the noise spectrum. This is the sound of a high-Q filter driving toward self-oscillation.
