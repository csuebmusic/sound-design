CSU East Bay · music

sound-design

Interactive teaching tools for courses in music synthesis, sound design, and digital audio. Each tool is a single self-contained HTML file that runs locally in the browser — drag sliders, toggle playback, and watch every visualization update in real time.

maintained by Inés Thiebaut · view on github

about

These tools are designed to make abstract audio-DSP concepts audible, visible, and interactive. Every file runs locally via the Web Audio API and Canvas, with no external dependencies.

The collection is organized around three large areas of the course:

  1. Digital Audio active
  2. Sound Synthesis planned
  3. Mixing and Signal Processing active

how to use

for students

Click any tool below to launch it. Each is meant to accompany a specific topic from the reading — use it while working through the chapter, not as a replacement for it.

for instructors / remixers

All files are MIT-licensed and self-contained — fork, adapt, or rehost freely. The visual language (warm palette, DM Mono + DM Sans, consistent metric cards) is shared across every tool so students can move between them without re-orienting.

Sinusoidal motion

02 tools

Foundational tools for the geometry of sinusoids — the vocabulary every later tool speaks.

phasor.html
rotating point → sine, cosine, sum
A rotating point on a circle traces sine and cosine as projections. Four animated panels — sine unrolling, cosine starting at its peak, the complex exponential on a single circle, and a time-domain sum of sin(θ) + cos(θ) that reveals itself as another sinusoid at 45°. Establishes the geometric foundation for z-plane, complex poles, and Fourier that the filter tools build on.
A·e^(jθ) = A·(cos θ + j·sin θ)
→
interference.html
two waves, independent amplitudes
Two sine waves at the same frequency with independent amplitudes A₁ and A₂, combined under a variable phase offset φ. A phasor addition diagram, a wave canvas showing both signals and their sum, and a live classification as constructive, partial, or destructive. Framed by a real-world scenario — two mics on one source — with schematic doodles of the 3:1 placement rule.
R = √(A₁² + A₂² + 2·A₁·A₂·cos φ)
→

Digital filters

05 tools

Interactive tools covering the foundations of recursive (IIR) and non-recursive (FIR) digital filtering, designed to accompany Puckette (Ch. 8) and Roads (Ch. 28), among other standard texts.

IIR · recursive
one_pole_iir.html
single pole on the real axis
The simplest recursive digital filter. A single pole slides along the real axis of the z-plane — from lowpass (r > 0) through neutral to highpass (r < 0), all the way to r = ±1 where the pole lands on the unit circle and gain becomes infinite. Hear the filter on white noise while impulse response, frequency response, and group delay update together.
y[n] = g·x[n] + r·y[n−1]
→
two_pole_iir.html
complex conjugate pole pair · resonance
The building block of resonant filtering — a complex conjugate pole pair, the geometric object responsible for resonance. Impulse response rings exponentially, the frequency-response curve uses adaptive dense sampling to capture peaks up to Q > 1000, and group delay makes the frequency-dependent timing visible. Foundation for formant filters, plucked strings, and analog synth emulations.
b₁ = 2r·cos(ω₀), b₂ = r²
→
FIR · non-recursive
fir_avg_diff.html
N-tap moving average ↔ differentiator
Two sides of the same structure. Toggle between +1/N (all positive → lowpass) and (−1)ᵏ/N (alternating signs → highpass comb) to see how one sign flip rewrites the filter entirely. The impulse response equals the coefficient list exactly — the defining property of FIR. Group delay is a single number (N−1)/2 that applies uniformly to every frequency, the critical contrast with the IIR tools.
H(ω) = (1/N)·|sin(Nω/2)/sin(ω/2)|
→
fir_2_zero.html
complex conjugate zero pair · FIR notch
A 3-tap FIR with a complex conjugate zero pair — the FIR counterpart to two_pole_iir. Same z-plane vocabulary, but with zeros instead of poles. True notch behavior only when r = 1 (zeros on the unit circle); at r < 1 the zeros pull inside, creating a partial dip and a broader spectral tilt. Group delay goes to −∞ at the notch as r → 1, the FIR sign-mirror of the IIR's resonance spike.
a₁ = −2r·cos(ω₀), a₂ = r²
→
IIR + FIR · the biquad
biquad.html
two poles + two zeros · parametric EQ
The second-order pole/zero filter — the direct combination of two_pole_iir and fir_2_zero. Block diagram shows direct-form II with a shared delay chain firing left to feedback and right to feedforward. Poles (crimson ×) and zeros (teal ○) on the z-plane simultaneously for the first time. When freq_z = freq_p: equal radii cancel, pole-wins gives a peak, zero-wins gives a notch. The core of parametric EQ, visible directly.
y = g·(x + a₁·x₋₁ + a₂·x₋₂) + b₁·y₋₁ − b₂·y₋₂
→
